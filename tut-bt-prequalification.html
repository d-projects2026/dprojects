Running a Contractor Prequalification Assessment

Screen contractors before tendering to ensure only qualified firms compete for your project.

⏱ 14 min read📊 Intermediate

01Why Prequalify?

Prequalification is a risk management tool. By screening contractors before inviting them to tender, you ensure that every bid you receive comes from a firm with the financial capacity, technical capability, and track record to deliver your project. In Egypt's construction market — where contractor quality varies widely — prequalification is not optional; it's essential. It saves time (fewer bids to evaluate), reduces risk (no unqualified winners), and improves competition quality (bidders know they're competing against peers).

02Setting Up the Prequalification Template

1
Define Evaluation Categories
The D. Projects template includes five standard categories: Financial Standing (25%), Technical Capability (25%), Relevant Experience (20%), Organizational Capacity (20%), and HSE Record (10%). Adjust weights based on your project's risk profile.
2
Set Minimum Thresholds
For each category, define pass/fail criteria. For example: minimum annual turnover of 3x the contract value, at least 3 similar completed projects in the last 5 years, valid insurance with coverage equal to or exceeding the contract value, and no outstanding litigation above a certain threshold.
3
Prepare the PQ Document
The template generates a Prequalification Questionnaire (PQQ) that you send to prospective contractors. It includes sections for company information, financial statements, project references, equipment list, key personnel CVs, and HSE statistics.

03Evaluating Submissions

1
Financial Assessment
Review audited financial statements for the last 3 years. Check annual turnover trend (growing or declining?), current ratio (above 1.0 indicates liquidity), debt-to-equity ratio, and bank references. The template auto-calculates financial health indicators from the data you input.
2
Technical Capability
Evaluate equipment ownership vs rental (owned equipment shows commitment), workshop and storage facilities, in-house technical staff qualifications, and quality management systems (ISO 9001 certification is a strong indicator).
3
Experience Scoring
Score each reference project based on similarity to your project: same type (residential, commercial, infrastructure), similar value (within 50-200% of your contract), same region (local experience matters), and completed within the last 5 years. The template includes a similarity scoring matrix.
4
HSE Review
Check Lost Time Injury Frequency Rate (LTIFR), fatality records, HSE policy and procedures documentation, and training records. In Egypt, verify compliance with local labor law requirements for site safety.
PRO TIP
Request reference contacts for at least 2 completed projects and actually call them. Written references are easy to fabricate; a 5-minute phone call reveals the real story about a contractor's performance.

04The Shortlist Decision

After scoring all submissions, the template generates a ranked list. Typical practice is to shortlist 4-6 contractors for tendering. Fewer than 3 doesn't provide enough competition; more than 8 creates an evaluation burden. The template highlights contractors who pass all minimum thresholds and ranks them by total weighted score. Share the shortlist with your client for approval before issuing tender documents.

05Next Steps

With your shortlist established, proceed to Structuring a Winning Tender Response to understand what makes a strong bid — useful whether you're preparing tenders or evaluating them.
