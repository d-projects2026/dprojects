Documenting Defects and Non-Conformances

Properly record, classify, and track construction defects from discovery through resolution.

⏱ 14 min read📊 Intermediate

01Why Documentation Matters

A defect that isn't documented doesn't officially exist. In construction — where defects can have structural, safety, and financial implications — proper documentation protects all parties. Good NCR documentation provides the legal record for warranty claims, the data for identifying systemic quality issues, evidence for withholding payments or back-charges, and the audit trail for regulatory compliance. The D. Projects NCR system ensures every defect is captured consistently.

02Creating a Non-Conformance Report

1
Identification
Record what was found: location (building, floor, gridline, room), element (column, beam, wall, pipe), nature of defect (crack, misalignment, wrong material, missing component), and how it was discovered (routine inspection, hold point, visual observation, testing). Be specific — 'concrete crack in Column C3, 3rd floor, east face, approximately 0.3mm width, 450mm length' not 'cracked column.'
2
Classification
Categorize the severity: Critical (structural safety concern, immediate stop-work required), Major (does not meet specification, rework required before proceeding), Minor (cosmetic or minor deviation, can be rectified during finishing). The classification determines response urgency and escalation level.
3
Root Cause
Identify why the defect occurred: material issue (wrong grade, expired, damaged), workmanship (untrained labor, rushing, inadequate supervision), design (unclear detail, error, clash), or process (wrong sequence, missing hold point, environmental conditions). Root cause analysis prevents recurrence.
4
Corrective Action
Specify the required remedy: remove and replace, repair in-situ, accept with engineering justification (for minor deviations), or redesign. Include the method statement for the corrective work, who is responsible, and the completion deadline.
5
Verification
After corrective action, the original inspector (or another qualified person) re-inspects and records the result. The NCR is only closed when the verification inspection confirms the defect has been resolved to specification.
PRO TIP
Photograph every NCR — before, during correction, and after. A visual record is worth more than a thousand words of description, especially when disputes arise about whether correction was adequate.

03Tracking & Trending

The D. Projects NCR log aggregates all non-conformances with sortable fields for analysis. Look for patterns: same defect type recurring (systemic issue), same subcontractor generating most NCRs (performance problem), same building zone showing defects (supervision gap). The tracking template auto-generates trend charts for your weekly quality meeting. These trends feed into the Site Inspection Dashboard and ultimately the portfolio-level DD&A KPI Screen.
